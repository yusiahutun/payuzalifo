Is acupuncture better than Botox as an anti-ageing treatment?

From 'baby Botox' to facial acupuncture, Bazaar investigates the different expert skincare procedures that give you subtle youth-boosting results, so you can make an informed choice about how you can look your best for longer.

On botox

I remember my grandmother constantly telling me to stop frowning as a child. I wasn’t a cross little girl, but the two lines etched between my brows proclaimed otherwise. By my twenties, I was used to the cries of ‘cheer up, love’ that would echo from the building sites I passed, and to fielding concerned enquiries from good friends when I felt perfectly happy. At 29, when a bad break-up really had dented my joie de vivre, my boss at the time, Newby Hands, suggested that I try Botox. As she happened to be the beauty director of this magazine, and also the first person to write about Botox in the UK, I took her advice. I still think it is the best beauty decision I have ever taken.

Botox is perhaps today’s most ubiquitous entry-level cosmetic procedure. A medical neurotoxin originally developed to treat cross eyes, then used to combat excessive sweating and control muscle spasms in cerebral-palsy sufferers, it was swiftly repurposed by the beauty industry to paralyse the facial muscles that, used repeatedly, cause lines and wrinkles. The cosmetic-Botox business is now worth $3.2 billion a year worldwide; by 2020, it’s estimated that 1.5 million people in the UK will be opting for it and other such non-surgical treatments.

Aside from the prick of the needle, and a mild headache that one in four users will experience as the botulinum toxin kicks in over the following few days, it is painless. I have had Botox every six months for the past 13 years, because it means that my face, although still extremely expressive, now gives off the right signals. I look calm when I’m at peace, joyful when I’m happy. The scowl has gone, and with it, the dull ache that came from hours of scrunching up my brows.

Indeed, I believe Botox actually makes me feel happier, and not just because I’m more confident about my appearance. According to scientists, the ‘facial-feedback hypothesis’ means that the expression you adopt may result in mood-altering messages being sent to the brain. Adopt a frown, and feelings of anger or misery can be triggered, while smiling lifts your spirits. A 2015 study by the Journal of Psychiatric Research suggest that injecting Botox to inhibit those muscle movements most associated with low mood might in fact change the feelings that go with them, and perhaps even cure depression.

The trick with Botox is not to abuse it, in order to avoid the frozen-faced look that is instantly recognisable. Remember, this is a medical procedure with potential side effects. I always opt for ‘baby Botox’ – the tiniest amount, dotted between and along my brows – and avoid having it injected into my forehead. Plus, I only ever see the best doctors in the business, which is crucial, as, infuriatingly, it is still unregulated.

In London, I go to Dr Costas Papageorgiou at the Harrods Wellness Clinic, Dr Sophie Shotter at the Cosmetic Skin Clinic, and Dr Michael Prager at his Beauchamp Place practice. When it comes to finding your own cosmetic doctor, make sure that they are professionally registered and regulated by the GMC, NMC or GDC.

"The British College of Aesthetic Medicine is also a good place to find a doctor. If a clinic is CQC-registered then it is also a huge advantage as it means it has been inspected extremely thoroughly and adheres to strict standards. Registers such as Save Face also provide a form of inspection and ensure practitioners on their register are trained and insured," says Dr Shotter.

I also think it’s worth asking to see a doctor’s portfolio of work to ensure the results they achieve are as natural as you would like them. "Skilled Botox relies on understanding each patient’s individual anatomy and intricate mechanisms of muscle interaction with the skin," explains Dr Papageorgiou. "Under skilled hands, the treatment should have a very low complication rate. Any errors are usually related to a missed diagnosis during the initial patient evaluation."

When I was 29, my decision to have Botox wasn’t about looking younger, but fresher and happier. Now that I am 42, it is the same. I know there will come an age when I will stop using it, because beyond a certain point, it can make one look unnatural. But I have never wanted to be wrinkle-free; just to look as happy as I truly am.

Katy Young, group beauty director for Harper's Bazaar

On cosmetic acupuncture

A couple of years ago, I bumped into an acquaintance I hadn’t seen since we were at school together, and was so impressed by her smooth, glowing complexion and taut jawline that I couldn’t resist asking which cosmetician was responsible. To my astonishment, she insisted she had never been under the knife; her perfect skin was all down to regular acupuncture. Which is how, a month later, I found myself lying down in the consulting room of her chosen practitioner John Tsagaris, looking like Pinhead in the horror film Hellraiser, with around 100 needles stuck into my face and around my hairline, and a few more in my hands and feet.

Now in my late forties, I’m as anxious as anyone not to look older than I must; but I have seen too many frozen foreheads, lopsided smiles and over-elevated eyebrows not to be wary of Botox. Besides, the thought of injecting a potentially lethal toxin into the skin to paralyse the underlying muscle is undeniably off-putting. By contrast, acupuncture’s holistic approach makes sense to me. "The skin is the largest organ in the body," says Dr Tsagaris, a pioneer of beauty acupuncture in this country. "External signs of ageing may just be a reflection of an internal problem, such as a hormonal imbalance. Acupuncture is designed to address the cause of the problem as well as the symptoms."

Currently highly fashionable with health-conscious celebrities including Gwyneth Paltrow and Katy Perry, the technique itself has been used for millennia by practitioners of traditional Chinese medicine, in the belief that disease is caused by disruption to the flow of energy – qi – in the body, and that by stimulating particular points on the skin, the qi is released.

Sceptics are more likely to be impressed by the fact that acupuncture is available on the NHS, particularly for the relief of chronic pain, as the procedure has been shown to trigger the release of pain-killing endorphins, oxytocin and dopamine. "If I took a blood sample before and after acupuncture, it would indicate biological changes in your blood chemistry," says Dr Tsagaris. When it comes to the face, the micro-damage caused by the needles penetrating the skin causes the body’s healing processes to kick in, releasing anti-inflammatories and prompting fibroblast cells in the dermis to promote the production of collagen and elastin, both of which are naturally depleted by ageing.

Which is all very well, but does it hurt? For the most part, I scarcely felt it, though occasionally a needle elicited a deep, dull ache for a second or two, while others sent a jolt of sensation down my neural pathways. But Dr Tsagaris is immensely gentle, and once all the needles had been placed and I was lying under a blue LED to further activate my skin cells, a sense of relaxation and wellbeing washed over me and I dozed off. After half an hour, the needles were removed, a soothing moisturising mask applied, and I was offered a hand mirror to assess the results.

I took a look and burst into incredulous laughter. Staring back at me was a face I recognised from my distant past: plump, glowing, with the lines around my nose and eyes visibly shallower. Even the whites of my eyes seemed clearer, and when I returned to the office, several colleagues remarked on how well I was looking. No wonder Dr Tsagaris says it’s a popular treatment prior to photo-calls and red carpet excursions, for both men and women.

Ross J Barr is another highly regarded acupuncturist who counts the Duchess of Sussex amid his star-studded clientele. At his Golborne Road clinic, he took my pulse and quizzed me on my lifestyle before treating me for stress and an overheated liver, as well as for my nasolabial lines.

In contrast to my session with Dr Tsagaris, only a few needles were put in my face, but at the end of the session, learning that I was hosting a daunting event the same day, Barr used more needles in my hands and feet, twisting them around until I felt the zap of a nerve, when they were immediately removed. This ‘tonification’, he said, would strengthen my inner organs and give me an energy boost. And indeed, after 45 minutes, I emerged with taut skin, brighter eyes and, best of all, a completely transformed state of mind. I bounced into work, where I carried off the dreaded event with aplomb, and that night, slept deeply and soundly for the first night in weeks – an unexpected side-effect that I couldn’t have been more grateful for.

Meanwhile, the beneficial effects on my face seemed actually to increase as the days wore on. "Because we’re working at a deeper level, the skin should continue to improve during the days and weeks after," confirms Barr. "Acupuncture is not just a local treatment, it has the added benefit of someone feeling good after it as well. And naturally, when we feel good, our faces are brighter, less burdened and we look better anyway." Certainly, other more invasive treatments can make you look younger; but only acupuncture has made me feel that way too.

Lydia Slater, deputy editor of Harper's Bazaar
